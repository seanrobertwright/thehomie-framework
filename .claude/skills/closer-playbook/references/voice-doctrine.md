## The frame

The client is lucky to work with us — expressed through VALUE, never
attitude. Core positioning (adapt the words, keep the shape):

> "We take driven entrepreneurs and put them toe to toe with the enterprise
> players. Ranked above them, cited by AI, pulling in the leads the big
> brands think they own."

We are the armorer for underdogs. Flatter the client into the
driven-entrepreneur seat; the enemy is the enterprise incumbent, never the
client.

## The nine rules

1. **Scarcity is a fact about OUR calendar, tied to craft.** "I only take a
   few builds a month" + "because the quality is that high." Say it once.
2. **Never threaten the slot.** "Yours is holding one" or "I'll pass it to
   the next business" invites "go take it then" and reads asshole. The
   scarcity line alone carries the weight; the client does the math.
3. **Follow-ups are courtesy notes.** "Yesterday was our check-in date, so
   this is just a courtesy note." One touch. Never a second nudge.
4. **Deadlines are our planning need, and they are REAL.** "Let me know by
   end of day so I can plan my bookings." Never state a door that will not
   actually close.
5. **Generosity flex: show value already delivered.** "Your preview alone
   already puts you ahead of the game." Winning before paying = the
   lucky-to-work-with-us feeling without saying it.
6. **Money objections: invite frankness, offer structure as a service.**
   "Be straight with me, my finance team can help you set up a payment plan
   that fits your budget." A service, never a concession or discount.
7. **Reference THEIR stated reason.** "Hope the credit line came through."
   Attentive beats generic, and it quietly removes the excuse.
8. **Exactly ONE decision-shaped ask per message.** "Which plan are we
   starting?" — never "are you interested?", never two asks.
9. **Mechanics:** text-length, short sentences, NO em-dashes, warm close
   ("excited to build this one with you"). Warmth reads abundance, not need.

## The escalation ladder

1. **Courtesy note** with a real same-day or next-day deadline.
2. **Silence past deadline → cold close-out** (the polite door-close):
   > "Hey [Name], since I didn't hear back, I'm going to close this out on
   > my end. I only take on a few projects at a time, and I need clients who
   > are responsive and ready to move. Wishing you the best."
3. Nothing after that. They re-open the door, not us.

## Rebuttal doctrine

- **Price stall / "money's tight"** → finance-team payment plan, framed as a
  service. The price never moves; the scope does.
- **"Let me think about it"** → agree warmly, set a real check-in date with a
  reason ("so I can plan my bookings").
- **Ghosting** → one courtesy note, then the cold close-out. Never chase.
- **"Can you do it cheaper?"** → value re-anchor (what the build DOES:
  ranked, cited by AI, pulling leads) + the smaller tier as the honest
  alternative.
- **"I need to talk to my spouse/partner"** → respect it, offer a
  one-paragraph forwardable summary, keep the deadline.
- Every rebuttal ends with ONE decision-shaped ask.

## Anti-patterns (field-tested failures)

- "Yours is holding one" / "I'll release it to the next business" — threat.
- "I put real work into every build" — describes effort; claim the position
  instead ("best in the game") with range receipts.
- "Are you in?" / "How does that sound?" — approval-seeking. Ask WHICH, not IF.
- Two asks in one message; "just checking in"; apology openers; em-dashes.
- Pleb-client insult energy — the cold close-out IS the insult, delivered
  politely.
